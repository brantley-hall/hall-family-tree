Abstract

For decades, genealogical records incorrectly linked the pioneer Asa Hall of Montgomery County, Virginia, to an aristocratic Connecticut lineage. This 2026 revision demonstrates that the decisive evidence comes from Y-DNA haplogroup G-M201 (defined by the M201 mutation), which directly refutes the Connecticut hypothesis and points instead to the Rhode Island-to-New York Hall line documented by Wanda Ware DeGidio. The report is organized as follows:

  1. Definitive Y-DNA proof — the G-M201 haplogroup and why it rules out a recent Connecticut paternal ancestor.
  2. My Y-DNA result — how my own direct male-line test places me in the G-M201 Hall group.
  3. FamilyTreeDNA project evidence — how the Hall surname project confirms the same paternal signature.
  4. The 1970 DAR record and its spread — how a flawed 1970 lineage record, The Hall Family History Montgomery County, Virginia 1605–1922, was amplified through genealogical institutions and later preserved in my 2000 compilation.
  5. The real genealogical record — the land, probate, and lineage evidence that establishes the correct Hall story.

The result is a corrected, evidence-based account of Asa Hall, Jesse Hall, and the early Montgomery County Hall family.

Section I: Definitive Proof — The Y-DNA Haplogroup G-M201

The strongest evidence in this case is genetic. Paternal lineages are traced through the Y chromosome, which passes largely unchanged from father to son. Direct, documented male-line descendants of the Southwest Virginia Hall family carry Haplogroup G-M201, specifically the G2a subgroup. This is a discrete, inherited paternal signature.

By contrast, the Connecticut Hall families proposed as ancestors consistently belong to Haplogroup R-M269, the most common European paternal lineage and typical of many New England settlers. The two haplogroups diverged many thousands of years ago.

The implication is clear: the Montgomery County Halls and the Wallingford/Connecticut Halls do not share a recent paternal ancestor. Whatever institutional records once claimed, the Y-DNA profile makes the Connecticut hypothesis untenable. This finding is not a suggestion or a clue; it is a decisive genetic boundary.

Section IV: The 1970 DAR Record and Its Dissemination

The genealogical confusion began with a 1970 DAR lineage record titled The Hall Family History Montgomery County, Virginia 1605–1922. That record relied on the volatile methodology of surname-matching across distinct regional archives. It identified an attractive but undocumented Connecticut/Wallingford ancestry for the Montgomery County Halls and asserted it through institutional channels.

Once accepted, the record was copied, abstracted, and disseminated through:

  • DAR application files and published indexes,
  • county histories and family-history compilations,
  • online genealogical databases and family trees,
  • and my own 2000 compilation, A Slice of History: A Story of a Hall Family of America, which preserved several original documents, including the 1970 DAR record.

Because the assertion carried an institutional stamp, it was repeated as fact long after its source weaknesses were visible. The DAR record did not rest on continuous, locality-by-locality documentation. It rested on names that looked alike in different states.

Section V: The Real Genealogical Record

With the Connecticut hypothesis ruled out by Y-DNA and the DAR dissemination identified, the real Hall lineage follows the framework established by Wanda Ware DeGidio. Her research supplies continuous, locality-based documentation from Rhode Island through New York and then to the Virginia frontier.

The documented line runs:

John Hall I (Rhode Island)
  → Henry Hall II
    → James Hall
      → Joseph Hall
        → Benajah Hall
          → Asa Hall / Jesse Hall (Montgomery County, VA)

This New York residency explains Asa Hall’s Revolutionary War service in New York regiments (Captains Nathan Pierce and John Salisbury), including action at Long Island, White Plains, and Saratoga. It also explains why name indexes later confused him with unrelated Connecticut and Rhode Island soldiers of the same name.

Frontier Land Tenure

Following the war, Asa and Jesse Hall migrated to the Roanoke Valley around 1790 and immediately engaged Virginia’s legal land system. Their land patents demonstrate continuous, documented settlement:

150-Acre Patent — October 17, 1803

  • Issued by: Governor John Page
  • Authority: Land Office Treasury Warrant No. 3045, dated December 11, 1800
  • Location: Montgomery County, “on the waters of Kearnes run a branch of the north fork of Roanoke”

25-Acre Patent — April 1, 1815

  • Issued by: Governor Wilson Cary Nicholas
  • Authority: Land Office Exchange Treasury Warrant No. 1990, dated January 2, 1809
  • Location: “In the forks of Kenny a branch of the north fork of Roanoke”
  • Notable detail: The survey locates the boundary “beginning at two black oaks on a hill side just above his house on a line of his patented land.”

These entries serve as definitive proof of long-term stability. The legal text explicitly tethers Asa Hall’s physical residence—"his house"—to his expanding, contiguous acreage on Kearnes Run and Kenny Creek. They highlight an organized life centered around agricultural development and permanent community building.

Probate and Material Reality

The ultimate validation of a historical life rests in the probate records filed upon its conclusion. The estate files of Asa Hall, processed through the Montgomery County Court in the spring of 1841 (Will Book 6, Pages 325 & 326), provide an unvarnished view of his material existence. This record serves as the final rebuttal to the aristocratic myths of inherited New England fortunes.

On March 6, 1841, court-appointed appraisers John Birchfield, William Sublet, and Thomas Jewel conducted an inventory of the estate's personal property. The ledger reflects the utilitarian, self-sufficient lifestyle of an established Appalachian farmer:

  • Agricultural equipment & livestock: One cow ($6.00), one calf ($2.00), a spade and scythe ($2.00), a foot adz ($0.50), an ax ($0.75), and a lot of old iron ($0.50).
  • Domestic textiles & production: One little spinning wheel ($3.50), one big spinning wheel ($0.25), a “trambling sac,” and multiple beds with “riggin” and bedsteads.
  • Household goods: A cupboard ($10.00), an old clock ($9.00), a looking glass ($0.50), kitchen implements, and one gun ($6.00).
  • Intellectual & spiritual life: “Two books westey summond” (likely Methodist sermons of John Wesley) and “one lot of books,” indicating a literate household engaged with frontier religious movements.

On April 8, 1841, the court completed the dower allotment for the widow, Mary Hall. The description relies on permanent landscape features and immediate neighbors:

“Beginning at the line of James Grahams making a corner on two pices at said Graham lines on a ridge from there in a straight line to a walnut at the yard fence; from thence with the branch to John Jewels line...”

This legal description fixes Asa Hall's residence within a known community of named neighbors, further grounding his life in verifiable local records.

This 2026 revision places the decisive evidence where it belongs: in the Y-DNA haplogroup G-M201, confirmed by my own test and by the FamilyTreeDNA Hall project.
